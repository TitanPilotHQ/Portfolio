# Competition Intelligence — 01. Monitoring Scope

## What's tracked today

Module 04 (`04-positioning/02-competitive-positioning-map.md`) already
covers 8 real named competitors, organized into 5 clusters, sourced from
`COMPETITOR_COMPARISON` (`lib/content.ts:367-416`). That analysis is the
canonical positioning map — this file does not restate the competitor
names, cluster groupings, or differentiators. A reader who wants the actual
competitor list goes to Module 04.

This file's subject is different in kind: not "who are the named
competitors and how do we position against them" (Module 04), but "what
categories does Titan Pilot's monitoring scope cover, including categories
with no named competitor yet."

## Expansion source

Grounded verbatim in `INTAKE-REQUEST.md` §9.1 (Emad, 2026-07-12):

> Continue tracking existing governance and AI-assisted trading
> competitors, and expand monitoring to include emerging AI agent
> platforms, financial AI governance vendors, and enterprise
> decision-intelligence platforms as they become relevant.

The first clause — "continue tracking existing governance and
AI-assisted trading competitors" — refers to categories Module 04
already covers; it does not introduce new scope beyond what Module 04
owns. The operative new instruction is the second clause: expand
monitoring to three additional category types.

## The three new category types

Intake §9.1 names categories, not companies. No real or fabricated
company name is listed for any of the three categories below — the
category types themselves are the extent of what intake specifies. This
is a description of what each category type means at a general,
industry-standard level, not a competitor list.

**AI agent platforms.** Software platforms built around autonomous or
semi-autonomous AI agents that can take multi-step actions on a user's
behalf — planning, executing, and adjusting a sequence of tasks with
reduced or delayed human intervention, as distinct from a single-shot AI
tool that responds to one prompt and stops. Relevant to Titan Pilot's
monitoring scope because agentic architecture is directly adjacent to how
Titan Pilot itself operates in its AI-assisted trading modes.

**Financial AI governance vendors.** Vendors whose product is oversight,
auditability, compliance, or risk-control tooling specifically for AI
systems operating in a financial context — as distinct from general AI
governance tooling that isn't finance-specific, or financial compliance
tooling that isn't AI-specific. This category sits closest to Titan
Pilot's own accountability positioning (see Module 04's "Reading the
map" section on the decision-accountability axis, cited not restated
here).

**Enterprise decision-intelligence platforms.** Platforms that help
organizations structure, evidence, and audit decision-making processes at
an enterprise level — not limited to trading or finance, but relevant
where the underlying discipline (recording a decision, its inputs, and
its rationale in a reviewable form) overlaps with what Titan Pilot does
in a trading-specific context.

## On "as they become relevant"

*(Source: `OWNER-DECISIONS-2026-07-13.md` §4, Emad's direct decision,
quoted verbatim there; carried in `CLOSURE-REPORT.md` §5a as an owner
decision.)*

Trigger-only, plus quarterly monitoring. A competitor or category is added
only when a sourced development materially affects at least one of:

- Positioning
- Pricing
- Product differentiation
- Buyer expectations
- Regulation
- Distribution
- Partnership strategy

A competitor is not added merely because it uses AI or mentions trading —
the bar is a sourced, material effect on one of the seven areas above, not
category resemblance. See `03-governance.md` for how a real, sourced
addition gets handled when it happens.
